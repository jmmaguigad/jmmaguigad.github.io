Spot the Red Flags - Here's What the Email Looked Like

1
From: Google LLC
→ "Google LLC" is just a display name - check the actual sender email address. It may not be from a real @google.com domain
2
"Your password expires soon"
"Hi,"
→ Generic "Hi" greeting - no name, no account details
3
"…will expire in 24 hours"
"Expires: Mar 31, 2026, 11:59 PM"

Change password

→ 2-step fake flow designed to look identical to a real Google page, but the URL is wrong

Password expiry scams are one of the most common phishing techniques. Attackers use them to:

  • Steal your agency email credentials and use your account to send phishing emails to other employees or external contacts.
  • Access sensitive government data including beneficiary records, financial documents, and internal communications.
  • Launch Business Email Compromise (BEC) attacks from your account, potentially resulting in financial fraud against the agency.
Biggest Red Flag 🔗

Fake Reset Password Page with a Suspicious URL

The "Change password" button led to a page that looked identical to a real Google Reset Password page but the URL in the address bar was not accounts.google.com. Always check the URL before entering your credentials - a wrong URL means it's a fake page designed to steal your password.

⏰

24-Hour Deadline

"Expires in 24 hours" + "Mar 31, 2026, 11:59 PM" - attackers use extreme urgency so you act before you think.

👤

Generic "Hi" Greeting

Just "Hi" with no name. Real notifications from your IT admin include your full name and account details.

🔗

External "Change Password" Button

Real Google Workspace password changes are done through myaccount.google.com or the Google Admin Console - never through email links.

🔒

Fake Google Reset Password Page

The page looked identical to a real Google Reset Password page (2-step: enter email, then password) - but the URL in the address bar was not accounts.google.com. Always check the URL before entering credentials.

1

Always check the sender's actual email address

"Google LLC" as a display name means nothing - anyone can set that. Click or tap on the sender name to reveal the actual email address, and verify it ends with @google.com or @accounts.google.com.

2

Change your password through official channels only

Go directly to myaccount.google.com and change your password under Security settings. You never need to click an email link to change your password.

3

Always verify the URL before entering credentials

Before entering your password, check that the URL in the address bar is exactly accounts.google.com. Phishing pages use look-alike URLs that are slightly different from the real one.

4

Don't panic over "urgent" emails

Urgency is phishing's best friend. Take a deep breath and verify before you act. No real IT system locks you out instantly.

5

Report suspicious emails to RICTMS immediately

Forward to jmmmaguigad@dswd.gov.ph or call the RICTMS Help Desk. Don't delete the email - it's needed for investigation.

What If This Was a Real Attack?

If you had entered your real password on a genuine phishing page, here's what you should do immediately:

1

Change your password immediately

Go to myaccount.google.com and change your password immediately under Security settings.

2

Notify RICTMS

Call the RICTMS Help Desk and report what happened.

3

Check account activity

Review your recent email login history and look for any unauthorized access or sent messages.

4

Preserve the email

Don't delete the phishing email - forward it to jmmmaguigad@dswd.gov.ph for investigation.

You're not alone - and this isn't your fault!

Password expiry emails are among the most effective phishing lures because it's natural to fear losing access to your account. It's not surprising that many employees click on them.

What matters is: now you know how to spot them. Next time you get an "urgent password" email, remember to pause, verify, and don't click!
